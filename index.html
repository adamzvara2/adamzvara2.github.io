Article 01 ▶

What Hydrogen Is (and Isn’t): A Practical Definition

Beginner Foundation 8–10 min read

Hydrogen is often discussed as a major part of the energy transition, but it is also frequently described in ways that are either too simplified or overly optimistic. A practical definition helps investors, municipalities, and engineers evaluate projects with realistic expectations.

Practical context: In energy and industrial applications, “hydrogen” almost always means molecular hydrogen (H₂) — a colorless gas made of two hydrogen atoms. In practice, you handle H₂ in pipelines, vessels, compressors, and dispensers, not isolated hydrogen atoms.

Why this matters

Understanding what hydrogen is (and is not) directly impacts project feasibility, infrastructure decisions, and the credibility of sustainability claims. A clear definition helps determine where hydrogen creates real value and where direct electrification may be the better route.

  • Project feasibility: identifying where hydrogen solves a real operational problem.
  • Infrastructure choices: production, storage, compression, delivery, and safety architecture.
  • Sustainability claims: understanding what “green” hydrogen actually depends on.

The core definition

The most important point is that hydrogen is not a primary energy source. It is mainly an energy carrier, which means it stores and transports energy that originally comes from somewhere else.

If hydrogen is produced by electrolysis, the primary input is electricity. If it is produced from natural gas, the primary input is the chemical energy in the gas (plus additional energy for processing, and in some cases capture and compression steps).

A useful analogy is electricity: it is also an energy carrier. Hydrogen is similar, except it stores energy in chemical form and can be transported, stored, and used later.

What makes hydrogen attractive

Hydrogen can carry a lot of energy per unit mass. Using the lower heating value (LHV), hydrogen contains about 120 MJ/kg, and using the higher heating value (HHV), about 142 MJ/kg. This high mass-based energy content makes hydrogen relevant in applications where weight matters or where energy must be stored and moved in molecular form.

It is also versatile in end use: hydrogen can be used as a feedstock (chemicals, refining), as a fuel (combustion or fuel cells), and as an energy buffer for harder-to-electrify sectors.

What makes hydrogen challenging (and why infrastructure matters)

Hydrogen is extremely light, so its volumetric energy density is low under normal conditions. To store useful quantities, hydrogen usually needs to be compressed (e.g. hundreds of bar), liquefied (LH₂), or converted into another carrier such as ammonia or LOHC.

This single fact drives much of real-world hydrogen complexity: more equipment, higher CAPEX, additional energy losses, and larger footprints than many people initially expect.

Safety is another key engineering topic. Hydrogen has a wide flammability range in air and a low ignition energy compared with many fuels, which means systems must be deliberately engineered for leak prevention and controlled response. In practice that includes ventilation, leak detection, hazardous area classification, ignition source control, material compatibility, and emergency shutdown logic.

Hydrogen is not one thing: the pathway matters

When someone says “hydrogen is clean,” the correct response is: it depends on how it is produced and delivered. Emissions and energy use vary based on production route, electricity carbon intensity, upstream methane emissions (for fossil-based pathways), compression or liquefaction energy, and transport logistics.

A more precise statement is: hydrogen can enable low-carbon energy services if the full pathway is designed and operated accordingly.

Common misconceptions (practical corrections)

  • “Hydrogen is an energy source” → In most energy-system contexts, it is primarily an energy carrier.
  • “Hydrogen always beats batteries” → Batteries often win when direct electrification is feasible and efficiency matters most.
  • “Hydrogen is too dangerous to use” → Hydrogen has different hazard characteristics, but it can be handled safely with proper engineering and operational discipline.
  • “Hydrogen is automatically clean” → Climate impact depends on the full production and delivery chain.

Practical conclusion

Hydrogen is a flexible energy carrier and industrial molecule with strong value in specific applications, but it also comes with real engineering constraints driven by storage, safety, and the energy cost of production and delivery. When projects are designed around those facts, hydrogen solutions can be technically solid and credible.

Why Hydrogen Matters in Europe: Decarbonisation Roles Without Exaggeration

Beginner Strategy EU Context

Hydrogen is not a solution for every problem and it does not replace electrification. In Europe, hydrogen matters because it can reduce emissions in specific sectors where other options are difficult, expensive, or slow to scale.

Practical framing: Hydrogen is most valuable when it solves a defined decarbonisation problem better than the alternatives — not when it is treated as a universal replacement for electricity.

Why Europe focuses on hydrogen (practical reasons)

  • Difficult-to-electrify sectors: industrial feedstocks, some high-temperature heat processes, selected heavy-duty transport use-cases, and long-duration/seasonal storage concepts.
  • Energy security and resilience: hydrogen is part of EU thinking on reducing fossil import dependence and strengthening system flexibility.
  • Deployment policy and targets: EU plans and regulations already shape real investment pathways, including production and infrastructure targets.

Hydrogen is not always the best option, but in some areas it may be one of the few scalable options available within EU decarbonisation timelines.

Where hydrogen fits best in Europe

Role A — Replacing fossil-derived hydrogen in industry (strong case): Hydrogen is already used in refining, ammonia, and other industrial processes. Replacing fossil-derived hydrogen with renewable or low-carbon hydrogen can deliver direct, measurable emissions reductions when the production pathway is clearly defined.

Role B — Industrial decarbonisation beyond feedstock (selective case): Hydrogen can support some high-temperature heat applications and processes where a molecule is needed for chemical reasons (for example as a reducing agent). Where direct electrification is technically feasible and affordable, it often remains the first choice.

Role C — Heavy-duty mobility and corridor refuelling (specific case): Hydrogen can make sense when vehicles operate long hours, require fast refuelling, and follow predictable routes. This is especially relevant for corridor-based deployment strategies.

Role D — Long-duration storage and system balancing (limited but important): Hydrogen can support renewable-heavy grids through longer-duration flexibility and Power-to-X pathways, even though round-trip efficiency is usually lower than direct electrification or batteries.

Where hydrogen is usually not the best option

Hydrogen is often not the preferred solution for space heating in many EU regions, most passenger cars, or short-distance low-utilisation applications where charging infrastructure and charging time are manageable. In those cases, direct electrification often wins on efficiency, simplicity, and delivered energy cost.

EU credibility rules: definitions and certification matter

In Europe, claims about “renewable hydrogen” increasingly depend on compliance with EU definitions and certification pathways (including RFNBO-related requirements). For project developers, this means electricity sourcing strategy and documentation can be just as important as electrolyser technology selection.

Practical summary

Hydrogen matters in Europe because it can reduce emissions where industry needs a molecule, support selected difficult-to-electrify use-cases, and contribute to flexibility in a more renewable power system. It is most credible and valuable when applied to the right problems with clear technical and regulatory framing.

Material Selection for Hydrogen Service at Ambient Temperature

Intermediate Materials Engineering

In hydrogen projects, it is easy to focus on pressure, flow rate, and purity. In practice, long-term reliability is often decided by material selection — especially for pressure-containing parts, valves, fittings, and welded joints.

Key engineering point: At ambient temperature, the main concern is often not classic corrosion, but hydrogen-assisted damage — mechanisms where hydrogen entering a metal reduces ductility/toughness and supports crack initiation or growth.

The core concept: hydrogen can weaken metals without visible corrosion

Many problematic mechanisms start with atomic hydrogen, not molecular hydrogen (H₂). Atomic hydrogen can be absorbed by steels and diffuse through the metal. This means hydrogen service risk depends not only on pressure, but also on moisture, contaminants, surface condition, and operating behavior.

A system can appear clean and dry externally while still creating conditions that increase hydrogen entry into stressed metal — and therefore long-term cracking risk.

What increases risk in practice

Hydrogen-assisted damage becomes more likely when three factors combine: hydrogen entry, tensile stress (applied or residual), and a susceptible material structure.

  • Environment: moisture and contaminants can support surface reactions that increase hydrogen entry.
  • Stress state: residual welding stresses, forming stresses, and stress concentrators (threads, sharp transitions) often become crack initiation sites.
  • Material condition: microstructure, inclusions, hardness gradients, and fabrication quality strongly influence susceptibility.

Steel cleanliness and microstructure matter

In steels, microstructural features and non-metallic inclusions can act as crack initiation points. That is why material selection cannot be reduced to “pick a yield strength.” For some components, procurement requirements related to material quality and through-thickness performance may be just as important as strength and impact toughness.

Welds and heat-affected zones: common weak points

Weld regions require special attention because welding modifies microstructure, creates hardness gradients, and introduces residual stress. Even if the base material is acceptable, the weld and heat-affected zone can become the weak link in hydrogen service.

In practice, material compatibility and welding practice must be treated as a single engineering decision: procedure qualification, heat input control, filler selection, and fabrication consistency can be as important as the nominal material grade.

What good selection looks like (project method)

A practical method is to divide the system into risk zones. High-pressure sections, cyclic sections, threaded/notched geometries, small-bore components, and welded transitions should be treated as high-attention areas.

Many projects use austenitic stainless steels for critical piping, instrumentation, and high-integrity small-bore components. Carbon steels can also be used in defined conditions when code requirements, strength levels, fabrication quality, and inspection practices are appropriately controlled.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Choosing materials based on corrosion resistance alone while ignoring hydrogen-assisted cracking mechanisms.
  • Assuming base material performance automatically guarantees weld/joint performance.
  • Specifying higher strength “for safety” without considering increased susceptibility in many steels.
  • Using a weak inspection strategy for welded and cyclically loaded areas.

Practical checklist for design and procurement notes

Define the operating envelope first: pressure range, cycle frequency, minimum/maximum metal temperature, hydrogen purity, expected contaminants, and joining methods. Then identify high-risk zones and set fabrication and inspection rules that match those risks.

Material selection becomes much more reliable when it is tied to service conditions, weld quality control, and the loading history of the component — not only to a material grade on paper.
